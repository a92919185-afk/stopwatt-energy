# COUNTRY.md — Language & Cultural Adaptation Guide
## Read before writing copy for any non-US page. Read before auditing any non-US page.

---

## Overview

Every market in this system has a different emotional contract with supplement advertising.
What converts in the US can repel in Germany. What works in Australia is too soft for the US.
This document gives you the exact vocabulary, tone, spelling rules, legal constraints, and
adaptation checklist for each target market.

**Always identify the target country from the `slug` field before writing any copy.**
Format: `CC-productname` where `CC` is the country code (us, uk, ca, au, de, ie, nl, dk, se).

---

## PART 1 — MARKET PROFILES

### 🇺🇸 United States (us)
**Language:** en  
**Currency:** USD ($)  
**Regulatory body (disclaimer):** FDA/FTC  

**Emotional contract:** The US BOFU buyer has been sold to aggressively their whole life. They expect directness. Hesitation in copy reads as lack of confidence. Bold claims (with backing) convert.

**Tone:** Direct, benefit-first, urgency-heavy, emotionally immediate.

**Vocabulary:**
- Pain framing: Aggressive agitation is accepted ("robbing you of", "silently destroying")
- CTA style: Imperative, reward-focused ("Claim Your Discount", "Get My 50% Off Now")
- Social proof: "50,000 Americans", "customers across all 50 states"
- Time references: "within weeks" not "within a fortnight"
- Measurements: pounds, miles, fluid ounces (not metric)
- Spelling: color, favorite, organize, fiber, center

**What works:**
- "Finally, something that actually works" (implies everything else failed)
- Countdown-style urgency framing around the discount
- Price shown prominently with contrast ("regularly $99, today just $49")
- Reviews with city + state ("Sarah M., Austin, TX")

**What to avoid:**
- British spellings anywhere
- Passive or hedging tone ("you might notice")
- Academic/clinical language without a benefit bridge
- Overly formal sentence structure

**Legal (FDA/FTC — US only):**
```
* These statements have not been evaluated by the Food and Drug Administration.
[Product] is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease.
Individual results vary. Paid promotion.
```

### 🇬🇧 United Kingdom (uk)
**Language:** en-GB  
**Currency:** GBP (£)  
**Regulatory body (disclaimer):** MHRA  

**Emotional contract:** UK buyers are skeptical of American-style hard sell. They respond to confidence, not hype. Undersell slightly — let the proof do the work.

**Tone:** Confident but measured, slightly dry. Avoid overdramatising.

**Vocabulary / Spelling — non-negotiable:**
- colour (not color), favourite (not favorite), organised (not organized)
- flavour, honour, neighbour (not flavor, honor, neighbor)
- centre (not center), fibre (not fiber), programme (not program)
- realise, recognise, analyse (not realize, recognize, analyze)
- "brilliant" / "sorted" / "knackered" / "chuffed" — acceptable colloquialisms
- Avoid: "awesome", "amazing", "knocked it out of the park"

**What works:**
- Understated confidence ("Customers consistently report clearer vision after 4 weeks")
- Science-forward claims (UK buyers respect credentials)
- Softer urgency ("Today's offer includes 50% off — while current stock allows")
- Reviews with city + country ("James T., Manchester, England")

**What to avoid:**
- American idioms ("killing it", "crushing it", "slaying")
- Fake urgency ("only 3 left!") — UK consumers react negatively to pressure
- Excessive exclamation marks
- Price shown as USD — must be GBP (£)

**Legal (MHRA — UK only):**
```
* These statements have not been evaluated by the Medicines and Healthcare products Regulatory Agency (MHRA).
[Product] is a food supplement and is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease.
Always read the label. Food supplements should not replace a varied and balanced diet.
Individual results vary. Paid promotion.
```

**Additional UK legal rule:** "Clinically proven" requires a published, peer-reviewed RCT. Do not use this phrase without a citation.

### 🇦🇺 Australia (au)
**Language:** en-AU  
**Currency:** AUD (A$)  
**Regulatory body (disclaimer):** TGA  

**Emotional contract:** Australians are highly sceptical of marketing. They call out "wank" when they see it. The copy must feel honest, matter-of-fact, and anti-hype. Let reviews and facts carry the weight.

**Tone:** Casual, straight-talking, unpretentious. Anti-hype. "No bull" energy.

**Vocabulary / Spelling:**
- Same as UK: colour, flavour, recognise, organise, centre
- "mate" and "reckon" are acceptable but use sparingly
- "value for money" resonates strongly
- Avoid: superlatives without hard evidence ("most powerful", "best formula ever")

**What works:**
- Social proof numbers ("Trusted by 40,000+ Australians")
- Simple benefit statements with no fluff
- Guarantee presented prominently (Australians are risk-averse purchasers)
- Reviews with suburb + state ("Helen K., Brisbane, QLD")

**What to avoid:**
- Over-dramatised pain agitation (reads as American manipulation)
- Buzzwords without substance
- USD pricing — must be AUD (A$)

**Legal (TGA — Australia only):**
```
* This product is a food supplement and has not been evaluated by the Therapeutic Goods Administration (TGA).
[Product] is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease.
Always read the label and follow directions. Individual results vary. Paid promotion.
```

### 🇨🇦 Canada (ca)
**Language:** en-CA  
**Currency:** CAD (CA$)  
**Regulatory body (disclaimer):** Health Canada  

**Emotional contract:** Similar to the US but slightly more reserved. Canadian consumers appreciate politeness and are less receptive to aggressive urgency.

**Tone:** Direct but warmer than US. Less pressure-heavy.

**Vocabulary / Spelling:**
- Mix of US and UK: "colour" and "favour" are correct in Canada
- "organize" and "recognize" are also acceptable (Canadian English is inconsistent — default to US spellings if unsure)
- Avoid very strong regional US expressions

**What works:**
- Same structure as US pages with softer urgency language
- Price shown in CAD (CA$49, not $49)
- Reviews with city + province ("Paul R., Vancouver, BC")

**What to avoid:**
- Prices in USD without a CAD equivalent
- Very US-specific cultural references
- Ignoring the Québec French market if targeting Canada broadly (EN pages target English Canada only)

**Legal (Health Canada):**
```
* This product is a Natural Health Product. This statement has not been reviewed by Health Canada.
[Product] is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease.
Individual results vary. This is a paid promotional website operated by an independent distributor.
```

### 🇮🇪 Ireland (ie)
**Language:** en-IE  
**Currency:** EUR (€)  
**Regulatory body (disclaimer):** HPRA / EFSA  

**Emotional contract:** Similar to UK — skeptical of American-style hype. Friendly and conversational tone is more effective than aggressive sales language.

**Tone:** Warm, conversational, grounded. Similar to UK but slightly more informal.

**Vocabulary / Spelling:**
- UK spelling rules apply: colour, favourite, organised
- "grand", "brilliant", "sorted" are culturally appropriate
- Avoid American idioms

**What works:**
- Same structure as UK pages
- Price in EUR (€49, not $49)
- Reviews with county ("Mary B., Cork, Ireland")

**Legal (EFSA — Ireland as EU member):**
Use the EU/EFSA disclaimer (same as Germany/Netherlands/Denmark/Sweden).

### 🇩🇪 Germany (de)
**Language:** de  
**Currency:** EUR (€)  
**Regulatory body (disclaimer):** EFSA  

**Emotional contract:** German consumers are the most skeptical of all target markets. They are highly educated, demand scientific backing, and distrust marketing superlatives. Logic must come before emotion. The mechanism must be explained before the benefit is promised.

**Tone:** Formal, factual, science-first. Emotion is secondary. Never hyperbolic.

**The critical rule:** ALL copy must be in German. No English words unless they are established product names (brand names are acceptable in English). "Denglish" (German mixed with English buzzwords) destroys credibility.

**Structure adjustment for Germany:**
- Lead with ingredients and mechanism, not outcome promise
- Hero headline: factual claim, not emotional aspiration
- Benefits section: start with the mechanism, then the outcome — not the other way around
- Reviews: focus on measurable outcomes, not emotional testimony

**Vocabulary guidance:**
- Formal "Sie" form in body copy; "du" may be used in CTA if the brand uses casual address
- Avoid: "amazing", "incredible", "unglaublich" overused
- Use: "wissenschaftlich untersucht" (scientifically studied), "klinisch getestet" (clinically tested)
- Legal: ONLY use EFSA-approved health claims. No claims that imply disease treatment.

**EFSA-approved phrasing examples:**
- ✓ "Vitamin A contributes to the maintenance of normal vision"
- ✓ "Zinc contributes to the maintenance of normal vision"
- ✗ "Restores your vision to 20/20" (disease treatment claim — prohibited in EU)

**Legal (EFSA — Germany):**
```
* Dieses Produkt ist ein Nahrungsergänzungsmittel. Nahrungsergänzungsmittel sind kein Ersatz für eine ausgewogene Ernährung.
Die Angaben auf dieser Seite wurden von der Europäischen Behörde für Lebensmittelsicherheit (EFSA) nicht bewertet.
Dieses Produkt ist nicht dazu bestimmt, Krankheiten zu diagnostizieren, zu behandeln, zu heilen oder zu verhindern.
Individuelle Ergebnisse können variieren. Bezahlte Werbung.
```

### 🇳🇱 Netherlands (nl)
**Language:** nl  
**Currency:** EUR (€)  
**Regulatory body (disclaimer):** EFSA  

**Emotional contract:** Dutch consumers are pragmatic, direct, and value-focused. They distrust marketing language but respond to factual, no-nonsense copy. Price-value framing works well.

**Tone:** Direct, honest, pragmatic. Skip the emotional build-up.

**Language:** Full Dutch. No English unless it is a brand name.

**Legal:** Same EFSA disclaimer as Germany (translated to Dutch).

### 🇩🇰 Denmark (dk)
**Language:** da  
**Currency:** DKK (kr)  
**Regulatory body (disclaimer):** EFSA  

**Emotional contract:** Scandinavian consumers value authenticity and transparency above all. Any hint of manipulation causes immediate distrust.

**Tone:** Calm, transparent, science-backed. No urgency pressure.

**Language:** Full Danish. EFSA disclaimer in Danish.

**What works:** Strong guarantee presentation, third-party testing references, clean benefit claims.

### 🇸🇪 Sweden (se)
**Language:** sv  
**Currency:** SEK (kr)  
**Regulatory body (disclaimer):** EFSA  

**Emotional contract:** Similar to Denmark. Swedes are highly educated health consumers who research before buying. Social proof from "real people" works better than clinical claims.

**Tone:** Trustworthy, balanced, never pushy.

**Language:** Full Swedish. EFSA disclaimer in Swedish.

## PART 2 — CRITICAL NON-NEGOTIABLES BY MARKET

These are automatic BLOCKERs during audit for any non-US market:

| Market | Blocker |
|--------|---------|
| uk, au, ie | US spelling detected (color, favorite, organize) |
| de, nl, dk, se | Copy is in English (not translated) |
| de, nl, dk, se | Uses FDA or MHRA disclaimer instead of EFSA |
| uk | Uses FDA or MHRA disclaimer incorrectly (MHRA is the correct UK body) |
| ca | Prices shown in USD without CAD notation |
| de | Uses unapproved health claim ("restores vision", "reverses condition") |
| All non-US | Uses US FDA/FTC disclaimer |

## PART 3 — CULTURAL ADAPTATION CHECKLIST

Run this checklist when adapting any page from one market to another:

### Language
- [ ] All copy translated by a fluent speaker (not machine-translated without review)
- [ ] No untranslated English words except established brand names
- [ ] Country-appropriate spelling conventions applied throughout

### Currency and Pricing
- [ ] Price shown in the correct local currency with correct symbol
- [ ] Currency code correct: USD, GBP, EUR, CAD, AUD, DKK, SEK
- [ ] `price_currency` field in JSON updated to correct ISO 4217 code

### Legal / Regulatory
- [ ] Disclaimer updated to reference the correct regulatory body for the target market
- [ ] `og_locale` field updated (en_US → en_GB / en_AU / de_DE / etc.)
- [ ] Modal content reviewed for country-specific legal requirements

### Social Proof
- [ ] Review names sound culturally appropriate for the target market
- [ ] Review locations reference cities/regions in the target country
- [ ] Review count is plausible for the market size (don't use "50,000 Americans" on a UK page)

### Tone
- [ ] Pain agitation level adjusted for market (US: high, UK/AU: medium, DE/NL/SE: low)
- [ ] CTA language matches market expectations
- [ ] No culturally inappropriate idioms

### Content
- [ ] Measurements are in the correct system (US: imperial, UK/EU: metric where appropriate)
- [ ] Time references appropriate ("check-up" vs "check-up", "fall" vs "autumn")
- [ ] Any product images reflect the local market (flag icons, local certifications if applicable)

## PART 6 — EFSA-APPROVED HEALTH CLAIMS (EU/UK Reference)

For pages targeting Germany, Netherlands, Denmark, Sweden, or Ireland, only these types of claims are safe:

**Eye health:**
- "Vitamin A contributes to the maintenance of normal vision" ✓
- "Riboflavin (B2) contributes to the maintenance of normal vision" ✓
- "Zinc contributes to the maintenance of normal vision" ✓
- "Lutein and zeaxanthin help protect the eye from oxidative damage" ✓ (if product contains them)

**Antioxidant:**
- "Vitamin C contributes to the protection of cells from oxidative stress" ✓
- "Vitamin E contributes to the protection of cells from oxidative stress" ✓

**What to remove for EU pages:**
- "Restores 20/20 vision" → Replace with: "Supports normal vision function"
- "Reverses age-related decline" → Replace with: "Helps maintain healthy vision as you age"
- "Repairs damaged cells" → Replace with: "Supports cellular health"
- Any reference to treating, curing, or reversing specific conditions

## PART 7 — TRANSLATION QUALITY STANDARDS

When generating non-English pages (de, nl, da, sv):

1. **Use the JSON to define the structure** — write the content directly in the target language in the JSON fields
2. **Never machine-translate from an English draft** — write native first, then review
3. **If using AI translation assistance:** Always run a back-translation check (translate back to English, compare to intent)
4. **Cultural idioms must be replaced, not translated:** "knocked it out of the park" has no German equivalent — rewrite the concept entirely
5. **Formal vs informal address:**
   - German: "Sie" (formal) in body copy, "du" only if the brand explicitly uses informal register
   - Dutch, Danish, Swedish: informal "je/jij", "du" is standard in marketing
6. **Country-specific social proof:** If referencing customer numbers, localize ("50,000+ Deutschen" not "50,000 Americans")
