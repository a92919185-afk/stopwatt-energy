# Copy Documentation — BOFU Supplement Landing Pages
## For Claude Code, AI Agents, and Any LLM Writing Content for This System

> **The core rule of all copy in this system:**
> Every sentence must do one of three things: agitate the pain, prove the solution, or remove the risk.
> If a sentence does none of those three, cut it.

---

## PART 1 — WHY BOFU COPY IS DIFFERENT

These pages sit at the **bottom of the funnel**. The visitor already:
- Knows they have the problem
- Has probably tried other solutions
- Clicked an ad, which means they have purchase intent

This means:

| What to skip | What to lead with |
|-------------|------------------|
| Educating them that the problem exists | Acknowledging they've tried and failed before |
| Building awareness of the category | Differentiating THIS product from what they've tried |
| Explaining what a supplement is | The specific mechanism that makes this one different |

**The emotional state of the BOFU buyer:**
> "I've been dealing with this for a while. I've tried things that didn't work.
> I'm skeptical but I still want to believe there's something that works.
> Convince me this time is different — but don't waste my time."

Every section must speak to this emotional state, not to a cold traffic visitor.

---

## PART 2 — THE JOBS-TO-BE-DONE FRAMEWORK (Foundation of All Copy)

Before writing a single word, identify the **real job** the product is hired to do.

> "People don't buy supplements. They buy a version of themselves where the problem is solved."

The drill buyer wants a hole in the wall. The supplement buyer wants:
- More energy to play with their kids
- To stop being embarrassed by their body
- To feel like themselves again
- To avoid the fear of a condition getting worse

**Extract the real job from the testimonials on the official product page.**
The real job is always hiding in the emotional language of real customers, not in the ingredient list.

**Mapping the JTBD to copy:**

```
Functional job  → Features section (what the product does)
Emotional job   → Benefits section (how they'll feel)
Social job      → Reviews section (how others perceive them)
Anxiety job     → FAQ section (remove the fear of being wrong again)
```

## PART 3 — PSYCHOLOGY TRIGGERS BY SECTION

Every section of the page must activate specific psychological mechanisms. This is not optional — it is the architecture of persuasion.

### Alert Bar (top of page)
**Triggers:** Scarcity, Urgency, Social proof
- Badge 1: Discount urgency → **Scarcity** ("X% off — today only")
- Badge 2: Risk reversal → **Regret Aversion** ("60-day money-back guarantee")
- Badge 3: Social proof or shipping → **Bandwagon** ("50,000+ customers" or "Free shipping today")

**Rule:** The alert bar is the first thing seen. It must create a reason to keep reading in under 3 seconds.

### Hero Section
**Triggers:** Loss Aversion, Specificity, Present Bias, Mimetic Desire
- The headline must make the reader feel the cost of NOT acting (loss aversion)
- Numbers create specificity that makes claims believable
- "Present bias" — emphasize what they gain NOW, not in 6 months
- The hero badge (social proof) triggers mimetic desire: "50,000+ people like me already did this"

**Critical:** The hero is the highest-stakes copy on the page. If it fails, nothing below it matters. The visitor bounces in under 8 seconds.

### Trust Badges
**Triggers:** Authority Bias, Uncertainty Reduction
- GMP Certified, FDA Registered, Made in USA — these are authority signals
- They answer the unconscious objection: "Is this a real company or a scam?"
- They reduce cognitive load: the buyer doesn't have to evaluate safety — an institution already did

**Rule:** Only use badges that are true. A false badge (claiming FDA Approved when only FDA Registered) destroys trust when discovered and may trigger legal issues.

### Social Proof (Stats)
**Triggers:** Bandwagon Effect, Social Validation, Availability Heuristic
- "50,000+ happy customers" makes success feel achievable and common
- This section exists to shift the belief: "Maybe this actually works for people like me"
- Numbers should be the most specific allowed: "47,832 customers" is more credible than "50,000+"

### Benefits Section (The Problem)
**Triggers:** Confirmation Bias, Contrast Effect, Loss Aversion, JTBD
- This section should make the reader say "yes, exactly" at least twice
- Use their language, not clinical language
- Agitate first — let the pain breathe before introducing the solution
- The contrast effect: describe the "before" so vividly that the "after" feels like relief

**3-paragraph structure:**
1. The biological cause (clinical credibility)
2. The emotional/daily-life consequence (agitation)
3. The bridge to the product (hope + differentiation)

### Features / Ingredients
**Triggers:** Authority Bias, Specificity, Expertise (E-E-A-T)
- Features answer the rational objection: "Why should I believe it works?"
- Each ingredient must do two things: prove expertise (dosage, mechanism) AND connect to benefit (how you'll feel)
- "Which means..." is the bridge from feature to benefit

### Science Section
**Triggers:** Cognitive Ease, Authority, Narrative Persuasion
- Complex mechanisms explained simply = trust
- The 3-step structure creates narrative flow: problem → mechanism → result
- This section is for the skeptic who needs a logical framework before buying

### Reviews
**Triggers:** Mimetic Desire, Social Proof, Confirmation Bias, Availability Heuristic
- A review is only persuasive if it's specific: "lost 14 lbs" not "great results"
- The reader must see themselves in the reviewer: same age, same struggle, same desired outcome
- Location humanizes the testimonial — "Sarah M., Austin, TX" is more real than "Sarah M."
- Three reviews minimum; the pattern of success across multiple people is the actual persuasion mechanism

### FAQ
**Triggers:** Regret Aversion, Uncertainty Reduction, Authority, Commitment & Consistency
- Every question is a real objection. Treat it like a one-on-one sales conversation.
- The answer must fully resolve the fear — not deflect it
- Guarantee question must be answered with complete clarity and zero ambiguity
- The FAQ is the last chance before the CTA — it handles the buyers who were 90% convinced but had one lingering doubt

### CTA Buttons
**Triggers:** Present Bias, Action-Oriented Language, Zero Risk
- Action verb + what they get + qualifier
- Never: "Submit", "Buy Now", "Click Here"
- Always: something that describes the experience of clicking, not the transaction
- Example: "Claim My 50% Discount Today" beats "Buy Now"

## PART 4 — THE 7-SWEEP EDIT FRAMEWORK

Apply these sweeps **in order** to any section of copy before finalizing it in the JSON.

### Sweep 1 — Clarity
**Question:** Could a 14-year-old understand this without a dictionary?

Check for:
- Technical jargon without explanation ("bioavailability" needs "...which means it absorbs faster")
- Ambiguous pronouns ("it helps them with their results" → who is "them"?)
- Run-on sentences with too many ideas
- Unexplained abbreviations

Fix: Read aloud. If you stumble, the copy is unclear.

### Sweep 2 — Voice and Tone Consistency
**Question:** Does every sentence sound like the same person wrote it?

Check for:
- Shifts from conversational to clinical ("you'll feel amazing... the bioactive compounds...")
- Tone mismatches between sections (hero is bold, benefits section is timid)
- Country-appropriate language (US: "guys", UK: "folks" or neutral, DE: formal)

Fix: Pick one voice. Write everything as if speaking directly to one specific person.

### Sweep 3 — "So What?" Test
**Question:** Does every sentence answer "why should I care about this?"

Kill any sentence that:
- Describes a feature without a benefit ("Contains 400mg of Ashwagandha" → add "...which helps your body manage cortisol so you feel less overwhelmed by day 2")
- States a fact with no emotional relevance
- Sounds impressive to the writer but means nothing to the buyer

Fix: After any feature or claim, ask "which means...?" and write the answer. If the answer is weak, the original claim is weak.

### Sweep 4 — "Prove It" Test
**Question:** Is every claim substantiated?

Every superlative needs proof:
- "Most effective" → prove it with what data
- "Industry-leading" → leading by which measure
- "Clinically studied" → which ingredient, which study

The proof hierarchy for this system:
1. Named ingredient + dosage (strongest — verifiable from product page)
2. Real customer testimonial with outcome (second strongest)
3. Trust badge from a certification body (third)
4. Rating and review count (fourth)

**Never invent proof.** If a claim cannot be substantiated with real data from the official product page, remove the claim.

### Sweep 5 — Specificity
**Question:** Can every vague word be replaced with a number or a name?

Replace:
- "significant results" → "results within 2–4 weeks (based on customer feedback)"
- "many customers" → "over 47,000 customers"
- "high-quality ingredients" → "6 clinically-studied ingredients at therapeutic dosages"
- "great energy" → "sustained energy without the crash"

The specificity table for this system:

| Vague | Specific |
|-------|----------|
| results | lost X lbs / added X reps / energy by 9 AM |
| natural | no artificial fillers, no synthetic hormones |
| proven | studied in a double-blind trial / used by X customers |
| fast-acting | noticeable difference within X days |
| high dose | 500mg per serving (X× more than competing brands) |

### Sweep 6 — Emotional Texture
**Question:** Does the copy make the reader feel something?

Emotional texture is not manipulation — it is accuracy. The reader IS feeling these things. The copy must reflect reality.

Emotions to write into each section:

| Section | Target emotion |
|---------|---------------|
| Alert bar | Mild urgency / excitement |
| Hero | Hope mixed with skepticism ("finally something real") |
| Benefits | Recognition ("this is exactly what I've been experiencing") |
| Features | Confidence ("this is backed by real science") |
| Science | Understanding ("now I see WHY it works") |
| Reviews | Vicarious relief ("if it worked for her, it could work for me") |
| FAQ | Reassurance ("I'm not being foolish to try this") |
| Footer CTA | Commitment confidence ("I'm making a good decision") |

**For health supplements (YMYL): emotion must be truthful, not manufactured.**
Do not write fear of disease. Do not write false hope of miraculous results.
Write: the real emotional cost of living with the problem + the real relief of having it improve.

### Sweep 7 — Zero Risk at the CTA
**Question:** Have all barriers to clicking been removed?

Before every CTA, the reader must have seen:
- The guarantee (how many days, what exactly is covered)
- At least one trust signal (badge, rating, or testimonial)
- An answer to their most obvious objection (usually in the FAQ)

Near the CTA, include:
- Guarantee reminder ("60-day money-back guarantee")
- Risk statement ("No risk. No subscription. Cancel anytime.")
- Social proof callback ("Join 50,000+ customers")

## PART 5 — SECTION-BY-SECTION COPY FORMULAS

### Alert Bar Badges
```
Badge 1 (Urgency/Offer):    [icon] [X]% OFF — [qualifier e.g. Today Only / This Week]
Badge 2 (Guarantee):        [icon] [X]-Day Money-Back Guarantee
Badge 3 (Social/Shipping):  [icon] [X,000]+ Happy Customers  OR  Free Shipping on Orders $[X]+
```

Rules:
- Maximum 6 words per badge
- All three must be true and verifiable
- Urgency must be plausible — never "offer expires in 10 minutes" unless it does

---

### Hero Headline (`headline` + `headline_highlight`)

**Formula A — Loss Aversion:**
```
"Stop Letting [Problem] Rob You of [Desired Outcome]"
Highlight: "[Desired Outcome]"
```

**Formula B — Specificity + Benefit:**
```
"[X] Ingredients Designed to [Specific Outcome] in [Timeframe]"
Highlight: "[Specific Outcome]"
```

**Formula C — Identity/Future Self:**
```
"The [Audience] Supplement That Finally [Specific Result]"
Highlight: "[Specific Result]"
```

**Formula D — Contrast:**
```
"[Problem] Doesn't Have to Be Your [Life/Normal/Story]"
Highlight: "[Life/Normal/Story]"
```

Rules:
- The highlight must be the most emotionally resonant fragment — not the product name
- Must contain or strongly imply the primary keyword
- No exclamation marks
- Maximum 12 words total across `headline` + `headline_highlight`

---

### Hero Subtext (`hero_subtext_1` + `hero_subtext_2`)

**`hero_subtext_1`** — The bridge from problem to hope. 2 sentences max.
```
Formula: "[Audience] know the feeling — [pain point]. [Product] was built to change that."
```

**`hero_subtext_2`** — The product introduction with a differentiating claim. 2 sentences max.
```
Formula: "[Product] combines [X] [clinically-studied / science-backed] ingredients
in a formula designed to [primary mechanism of action]."
```

Rules:
- No buzzwords: "revolutionary", "game-changing", "cutting-edge", "innovative"
- Each sentence: active voice, one idea, under 25 words
- Subtext 2 must include at least one specific and verifiable claim

---

### Benefits Paragraphs (`benefits_paragraphs` array — 3 paragraphs)

**Paragraph 1 — The Root Cause (clinical, credible):**
```
Formula: "The reason [audience] struggle with [problem] isn't [surface reason] —
it's [underlying biological/chemical mechanism]. [Brief scientific explanation
using real terminology from the product page]."
```

**Paragraph 2 — The Emotional Consequence (agitation):**
```
Formula: "Over time, [biological problem] affects more than just [physical symptom].
[2–3 specific daily-life consequences: energy, relationships, work, confidence].
Sound familiar?"
```

**Paragraph 3 — The Bridge to Product (hope + differentiation):**
```
Formula: "That's why [Product Name] was formulated differently. Instead of [what
others do that doesn't work], [Product] addresses [root cause] directly, using
[X] ingredients specifically chosen for [mechanism]. <strong>[Key differentiating
benefit in bold].</strong>"
```

Rules:
- Paragraph 1: No emotional language — clinical tone establishes credibility
- Paragraph 2: Pure agitation — let the pain breathe, no solution yet
- Paragraph 3: The pivot — bring hope without overclaiming
- The `<strong>` tag is allowed in paragraph 3 to bold one key claim

---

### Feature Cards (`features` array)

```
Title:       "[Ingredient Name] ([dosage if available])"
Description: "[What it does biologically] — which means [what the customer
              experiences]. [One sentence on why this dose/form matters]."
```

Rules:
- Title = exact ingredient name from the official product page. Never invent.
- Dosage in parentheses if confirmed on the official page
- Description: 2 sentences maximum
- "Which means" bridge is mandatory — feature alone is not copy
- Icon: pick the most biologically accurate Font Awesome icon (fa-leaf, fa-brain, fa-heart, fa-fire, fa-bolt, fa-eye, fa-shield-halved)

**Examples:**

Good:
```
Title:       "Lutein (20mg)"
Description: "Filters high-energy blue light and protects the macula from
              oxidative stress — which means your eyes fatigue less after
              screen time. Clinical studies use this exact 20mg dose."
```

Bad:
```
Title:       "Lutein Complex"
Description: "A powerful antioxidant that supports eye health and vision
              wellness in a unique proprietary blend."
```
(No dosage, no mechanism, "wellness" is a filler word, no "which means" bridge)

---

### Science Steps (`science_steps` array — 3 steps)

```
Step 1: "[Mechanism name]: [What happens at a biological level — 1 sentence]"
Step 2: "[Result of step 1]: [How that changes what the customer experiences — 1 sentence]"
Step 3: "[Compounded outcome]: [The cumulative benefit of steps 1+2 — 1 sentence]"
```

**`science_result`:**
```
Formula: "The result: [specific, felt outcome] — [in timeframe or condition]."
```

Rules:
- Each step must be logically caused by the previous one
- No step can repeat the same mechanism as another step
- The result must be specific — "better eye health" is not specific
- Steps use clinical language; the result uses customer language

---

### Reviews (`reviews` array)

Each testimonial must have ALL of the following:
1. A specific problem they had before
2. A specific outcome they experienced
3. A timeframe (even approximate: "within a few weeks", "after 30 days")
4. A real-feeling human voice (not polished marketing language)
5. A name + location

**Formula:**
```
"I had been dealing with [specific problem] for [time period]. I tried [other things]
but nothing seemed to work. After [X weeks] on [Product], I noticed [specific change].
Now [specific positive outcome in their daily life]."
— [First Name Last Initial], [City, State/Country]
```

Red flags in reviews (disqualify if found):
- "This product is amazing and I highly recommend it" → no specific outcome
- No timeframe mentioned → feels invented
- Language is too polished → feels like marketing copy, not a real person
- No problem stated → no before/after contrast

---

### FAQ Pairs (`faq_items` array)

**Required 6 questions + answer formulas:**

**Q1 — Does it work?**
```
Answer: Lead with the most credible proof point (rating + review count OR clinical
ingredient data). Then acknowledge results vary. Then offer the guarantee as the
risk-free way to find out.
```

**Q2 — Is it safe?**
```
Answer: Manufacturing credentials first (GMP certified, FDA registered facility).
Then natural ingredients. Then recommend consulting a doctor if pregnant/nursing/
on medication. Never claim "completely safe for everyone."
```

**Q3 — How do I take it?**
```
Answer: Exact dosage instructions from the official product page. Timing (with/without
food, morning/evening). What to expect in weeks 1, 2, and 4.
```

**Q4 — What if it doesn't work for me?**
```
Answer: Full guarantee description. How many days. What exactly is covered.
How to claim it. Make this answer completely unambiguous — buyer hesitation
lives here.
```

**Q5 — Is there a subscription?**
```
Answer: Clear yes or no. If yes, explain how to cancel simply. If no, state it
explicitly. Subscription anxiety is one of the top conversion killers.
```

**Q6 — What's the best value option?**
```
Answer: Reference the multi-bottle discount (if real). Explain why the bundle
makes sense (supply consistency, per-unit savings). Include the CTA if the
template supports it.
```

## PART 6 — VOICE AND TONE BY COUNTRY

### United States
- **Tone:** Direct, confident, urgency-heavy, benefit-first
- **Vocabulary:** Everyday American English, no stiff formality
- **Pain framing:** Aggressive problem agitation is acceptable
- **CTA style:** Imperative, reward-focused ("Claim Your Discount")
- **Social proof:** "50,000 Americans", "customers across the US"
- **Avoid:** British spellings, overly clinical language without benefits

### United Kingdom / Ireland
- **Tone:** Confident but less pushy, slightly drier humor acceptable
- **Vocabulary:** "Brilliant" / "knackered" / "sorted" / "chuffed"
- **Pain framing:** Softer — acknowledge frustration without overdramatising
- **CTA style:** Action-focused but slightly softer ("Order Today — Risk Free")
- **Spelling:** colour, favourite, organised, flavour, centre, programme
- **Avoid:** American idioms ("knocked it out of the park"), excessive exclamation

### Australia / New Zealand
- **Tone:** Casual, straight-talking, anti-hype
- **Vocabulary:** Colloquial but not slang-heavy
- **Pain framing:** Matter-of-fact — Australians are sceptical of overselling
- **CTA style:** Action-forward, no fuss ("Order Yours Today")
- **Spelling:** Same as UK — colour, favour, recognise
- **Avoid:** Emotional manipulation, fake urgency — Australians call it out

### Germany
- **Tone:** Factual, rational, science-led — emotion is secondary to logic
- **Vocabulary:** Full German translation required — no Denglish
- **Pain framing:** Clinical explanation first, emotional consequence second
- **CTA style:** Direct but not aggressive ("Jetzt 50% Rabatt sichern")
- **Key difference:** Lead with ingredients and mechanism, not lifestyle outcome
- **Avoid:** Unverified superlatives — German consumers are highly sceptical of marketing claims
- **Legal note:** All claims must be EFSA-compliant

### Canada
- **Tone:** Similar to US but slightly more reserved
- **Vocabulary:** North American English, no strong regional expressions
- **Currency:** CAD, prices shown with CAD symbol
- **Avoid:** US-specific cultural references, ignoring French-Canadian market if targeting QC

## PART 7 — BANNED WORDS AND POWER WORDS

### Words to Never Use in Copy (Killlist)

| Category | Banned words |
|----------|-------------|
| Empty buzzwords | revolutionary, game-changing, cutting-edge, innovative, world-class, best-in-class, synergy, holistic, wellness journey |
| Vague intensifiers | very, really, truly, quite, somewhat, rather |
| Filler verbs | utilize (use), facilitate (help), leverage (use), optimize (improve) |
| Fake urgency | hurry, while stocks last (unless true), limited time (unless specific) |
| YMYL prohibited | cure, treat, diagnose, prevent disease, FDA approved (≠ registered), clinically proven (unless citing a specific study) |
| Passive voice | "results have been shown", "studies have found", "it has been reported" |
| Overused superlatives | most powerful, most effective, most advanced, best formula |
| Weasel words | almost, virtually, up to (in benefit claims), may, might (unless required for compliance) |

### Power Words for BOFU Supplement Copy

**Credibility words:** clinically-studied, peer-reviewed, formulated, standardized extract, therapeutic dose, GMP certified, third-party tested, double-blind

**Emotion words (allowed):** finally, relief, reclaim, restore, discover, transform, confident, energized, clear, sharp, stronger, lighter

**Specificity words:** exactly, precisely, specifically, in [X] days, per serving, milligrams, standardized to [X]%

**Action words (CTA):** claim, secure, get, start, try, join, discover

**Trust words:** guarantee, risk-free, no questions asked, backed by, verified, certified

## PART 9 — MODEL D COPY PARADIGM (Social Proof First)

> Model D is not just a layout change. It is a fundamentally different copy philosophy.
> The 10 patterns below were extracted from high-converting Model D pages.
> Apply ALL of them — not selectively. They reinforce each other.

### The Core Difference from Models A, B, C

Models A/B/C follow the traditional BOFU sequence:
```
Headline → Problem → Solution/Product → Proof → Close
```

Model D inverts the sequence:
```
Headline (with price) → Who's already buying it → What it is → How it works → Risk removal → Close
```

The copy in Model D must be written with this inversion in mind.
Warm BOFU traffic (from retargeting ads) doesn't need to be convinced the problem exists — they know.
They need to see that **people like them already bought this and it worked**, before they care about the mechanism.

---

### D-RULE 1 — Price in the Hero

Model D always shows price in the hero. The copy around the price must use **contrast framing**:

```
"Only $49" [large] — crossed out "$99" [smaller, crossed out] — "[X]% OFF Today" [badge]
```

The hero subtext acknowledges the price directly: "Get the same results at half the original price."
Don't bury or minimize the price. Show it proudly alongside the discount — the price conflict IS the hook.

---

### D-RULE 2 — Exoneration Hook

The hero headline and first subtext paragraph must exonerate the visitor.
Tell them their past failures were caused by an unknown mechanism — not their weakness.

```
Formula:
"Is your [symptom] getting worse, even with [what they already tried]?
It's not your fault. The real cause is [named biological mechanism]."
```

Exoneration removes shame (a conversion barrier) and replaces it with curiosity.

---

### D-RULE 3 — Restoration Language (Never Improvement Language)

| Never write | Always write |
|------------|-------------|
| supports | restores |
| improves | reclaims |
| boosts | reverses |
| helps with | gives back |
| enhances | rebuilds |

Restoration implies something was taken. It triggers stronger loss aversion than "improvement," which sounds like a nice-to-have.

---

### D-RULE 4 — Named Biological Enemy

Give the root cause a specific, clinical name. Never let it be vague.

| Vague | Named |
|-------|-------|
| "oxidative damage" | "free radical accumulation in macular tissue" |
| "aging eyes" | "progressive macular pigment thinning" |
| "screen strain" | "high-energy blue light penetration past the retinal barrier" |
| "low energy" | "mitochondrial ATP production decline after age 40" |

A named enemy gives the product's mechanism a precise target. The visitor's rational brain needs a problem to match the solution.

---

### D-RULE 5 — Reviews Before Education

Reviews appear in Section 3 — before ANY product explanation. Write them to stand alone.
A Model D review must work without the reader knowing anything about the product.

**The review must contain:**
1. A limiting belief the reader holds ("I thought declining vision was just part of getting older")
2. The belief-reversal moment ("Reticlear proved me wrong")
3. Specific sensory outcome ("Colors are brighter, text is clearer")
4. Emotional consequence ("I don't need to adjust my glasses anymore")

**Sensory specificity is non-negotiable.** Each review must name what the person saw, felt, or could do — not that the product "worked."

---

### D-RULE 6 — The "Imagine" Visualization Paragraph

Use `hero_subtext_3` for one visualization sentence + one anchoring sentence. Exactly 2 sentences.

```
"Imagine [specific sensory daily-life scene with the problem solved].
That [clarity/energy/relief] can be your new reality."
```

This is the only place to use "Imagine." Never repeat this pattern elsewhere on the page.

---

### D-RULE 7 — Mechanism Chain (Cause → Effect → Felt Outcome)

Each `science_steps` item must complete the chain:

```
[What happens biologically] → [what changes physically/structurally] → [what the customer experiences]
```

The chain is broken if it stops at the biological step. Complete it:
- Broken: "Lutein forms a macular pigment."
- Complete: "Lutein forms a yellow macular pigment that absorbs blue light — your eyes fatigue less by midday and recover faster overnight."

---

### D-RULE 8 — Guarantee Section as a Confident Promise

The `guarantee_text` is NOT a refund policy. Write it as a statement of confidence, not a defensive clause.

```
Formula:
"We stand behind [Product] with [X]-day guarantee. If you're not [specific positive outcome],
simply reach out within [X] days for a full refund. We're so confident in our formula
that we make this promise to every single customer."
```

Key word: "we're so confident" — the guarantee becomes proof of quality, not a safety net.

---

### D-RULE 9 — Benefits as Verbs

Every item in `benefits_list_items` must start with a verb:
- "Restores...", "Eliminates...", "Sharpens...", "Defends...", "Relieves...", "Boosts...", "Ensures..."

Each item = one concrete, felt outcome. Not a feature. Not a promise. An outcome.

---

### D-RULE 10 — Low-Friction CTA Language

CTAs describe the transformation, not the transaction.

| Transaction (avoid) | Transformation (use) |
|--------------------|---------------------|
| "Buy Now" | "Restore My Vision Now" |
| "Order Today" | "Claim My 50% Discount" |
| "Purchase" | "Try [Product] Risk-Free" |
| "Add to Cart" | "Get My Discount Now" |

The visitor who clicks a transformation CTA is moving toward something.
The visitor who clicks a transaction CTA is spending something. The psychology is completely different.
